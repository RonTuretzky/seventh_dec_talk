What's Already Happening

Multiple organizations are building collective living infrastructure in rural Japan right now:

Akiya Collective

Nonprofit in Nagano Prefecture. Renovating abandoned houses into creative residencies and makerspaces.

  • First property: Komoro Town (2024)
  • 100+ volunteers (carpenters, musicians, founders)
  • Open-source playbooks, contribute-to-stay model

akiyacollective.org

Zu City Japan

Residential community 90 min from Tokyo in the Japanese Alps. Combining deep work with rural living.

  • 310+ members, 2 homes owned
  • Coliving ($1.5K/mo) ‚Üí Ownership ($25K avg)
  • Co-working, community cars, shared services

Lived Experience

A few years in a desert commune. Art. Philosophy. Meditation. Shared labor. Internal economy without money.

What Worked

  • Actually belonging somewhere
  • Work that meant something beyond a paycheck
  • Children raised by the whole community
  • Meaning from contribution, not consumption

What Didn't

  • No coordination technology (still using paper)
  • Cut off from the outside world
  • No pathway to replicate elsewhere
  • Governance that didn't scale

The question: can that freedom be combined with modern coordination tools?

The Gap in Empires

Empires expand and contract. They can't control everything all the time.

There are always gaps‚Äîplaces that aren't worth occupying, places that get forgotten, places in transition.

Rural Japan is one of those gaps right now:

  • Depopulating villages with infrastructure already built
  • Aging owners looking for successors, not profit
  • Not strategically important to anyone

You don't contest power by building a bigger army. You find the places it doesn't care about, and you build there.

Two Models

Two distinct approaches to collective organization have emerged:

Network State Model

  • Exit-based governance ‚Äî fork if you disagree
  • Regulatory arbitrage; special economic zone logic
  • Founder-centric, shareholder-driven structures
  • Wealth gates at the door ($10K+ to participate)
  • Building parallel to existing communities

Coordination Model

  • Commons-based governance ‚Äî work through disagreement
  • Shared ownership of land and equipment
  • Democratic decision-making, no founder-kings
  • Open to contribution, not just capital
  • Building with existing villages, not colonizing

Akiya Collective and Zu City Japan operate in the coordination framework.

One model scales through capital. The other scales through contribution.

See: Collective Intelligence Project, De Filippi on CoordiNATIONS

Mutual Insurance

What if the commune had insurance? Not from a corporation. From each other.

The Mechanism

  • Multiple communes pool resources into a shared fund
  • Prediction markets (run by the communes) track regional risks
  • Human rights declining? Natural disaster? The insurance kicks in.
  • Relocation pathway to another commune in the network

Not hedging a portfolio. Hedging a life.

12 / 20

Insurance as Place

Traditional insurance gives cash. But cash doesn't help if there's nowhere to go.

The payout isn't money‚Äîit's a place to live and work. Real estate and community hold value in collapse scenarios. A check doesn't.

Not New

  • Mutual insurance societies existed for centuries before corporations ate them
  • Prediction markets consistently outperform expert forecasts
  • Cryptography makes cross-border pooling and coordination possible

Old ideas. New infrastructure.

Who This Is For

Collective living has selection effects. It works for some people and not others:

Likely Fit

  • Interdependence over independence
  • Physical labor doesn't scare you
  • Group decisions over unilateral control
  • Long time horizons (years, not months)
  • Building, not consuming

Likely Not

  • Need for complete autonomy always
  • Sharing things causes stress
  • Looking for a vacation home
  • Expecting someone else to build it
  • Seeking escape rather than project

Options exist: rural or urban, temporary or permanent, multi-generational or not.
