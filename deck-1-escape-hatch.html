What's Already Happening in Komoro

Komoro — a former post-town in Nagano Prefecture, 90 min from Tokyo, nestled between castle ruins and Mt. Asama. Two organizations are already building collective infrastructure here:

Akiya Collective

Nonprofit turning abandoned houses into art installations, residencies, and makerspaces. A social R&D lab for collective behavior.

  • Makerspace: 3D printer, bio printer, sewing machine, Starlink
  • Contribute-to-stay model — earn residency through work
  • Open-source playbooks and community experiments

Zu City Japan

Residential community for entrepreneurs, scientists, and designers. Deep work meets hot springs, skiing, and mountain air.

  • Shared kitchen, garden, coworking, community cars
  • Conferences, raves, and parties
  • Flexible paths from short stays to ownership

Lived Experience

A few years in a desert commune. Art. Philosophy. Meditation. Shared labor. Internal economy without money.

What Worked

  • Actually belonging somewhere
  • Work that meant something beyond a paycheck
  • Children raised by the whole community
  • Meaning from contribution, not consumption

What Didn't

  • Cut off from the outside world
  • No pathway to replicate elsewhere
  • Not fully manifested: globalized transient collectivism

The question: can that freedom be combined with modern coordination tools?

Shared Resources

What does coordination look like in practice?

A group of people owning a farm and some workshops together.

  • Shared equipment — tractors, fabrication tools, solar arrays
  • Shared labor — specialization without isolation
  • Shared risk — when one person has a bad year, everyone eats

Revolutionary? Or just how humans organized for most of history?

7 / 17

Two Models

Two distinct approaches to collective organization have emerged:

Network State Model

  • Exit-based governance — fork if you disagree
  • Regulatory arbitrage; special economic zone logic
  • Founder-centric, shareholder-driven structures
  • Wealth gates at the door ($10K+ to participate)
  • Building parallel to existing communities

Coordination Model

  • Commons-based governance — work through disagreement
  • Shared ownership of land and equipment
  • Democratic decision-making, no founder-kings
  • Open to contribution, not just capital
  • Building with existing villages, not colonizing

Akiya Collective and Zu City Japan operate in the coordination framework.

One model scales through capital. The other scales through contribution.

See: Collective Intelligence Project, De Filippi on CoordiNATIONS

8 / 17

Finding Your Form

Different forms of collectivism suit different people. The question isn't if, but which:

High Interdependence

  • Full income-sharing communes
  • Shared childcare and elder care
  • Consensus decision-making
  • Long-term land stewardship
  • Integrated work and living

Lighter Coordination

  • Cohousing with private incomes
  • Consumer cooperatives (REI, credit unions)
  • Buying clubs and tool libraries
  • Seasonal residency programs
  • Work alongside, live apart

The spectrum runs from "attend a potluck" to "merge your household." Both are valid.

Rural or urban. Temporary or permanent. Multi-generational or not. The form follows the need.

And for some people, none of this. That's also fine.

Mutual Insurance

What if the commune had insurance? Not from a corporation. From each other.

The Mechanism

  • Communes pool resources into a shared fund
  • Prediction markets track regional risks
  • When triggers hit, relocation pathway activates

The Payout

  • Not money—a place to live and work
  • Real estate holds value in collapse scenarios
  • Community is the insurance

Mutual insurance societies existed for centuries. Cryptography makes cross-border coordination possible. Old ideas. New infrastructure.

Not hedging a portfolio. Hedging a life.

The Skeleton

What's the minimum viable alternative?

  • A few industrial communes making actual goods
  • Coordinating their economies with cryptography — no banks, no middlemen
  • Insuring each other against regional collapse
  • Sharing workers, resources, knowledge across borders

That's the skeleton of something else.

Not utopia. Just: different from what exists.
