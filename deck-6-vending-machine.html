Let me tell you about a vending machine

Japan has more vending machines per capita than anywhere on Earth. 5 million of them. $60 billion industry.

They're everywhere. They work perfectly. They're also... kind of a metaphor?

Here's the thing: who owns them?

Corporations. Obviously. The workers who stock them, maintain them, collect the money? They get wages. The machine makes someone else rich.

What if it didn't have to be that way?

2 / 12

Mutual Vend: the mechanism

Worker deposits collateral = 2x machine cost
Revenue splits automatically via smart contract
No payment processor (saves ~50% of margins)
Machine pays for itself in [X] months
Then: machine replicates. Network grows.

No boss. No shareholders. No bank taking a cut. Just workers owning a thing that makes money.

This isn't a thought experiment. This is a business model.

3 / 12

Why this works (when other things don't)

Three conditions for post-capitalist business:

  • Better for workers than the capitalist alternative (you own it)
  • Viable without subsidies (it actually makes money)
  • Collectively owned without needing a state to enforce it (smart contracts)

Most "alternatives" fail one of these. Co-ops need legal structures. Communes need subsidies. Crypto projects need... crypto people.

This one threads the needle.

The industrial commune

Not a hippie commune. Not a retreat. A productive unit.

What it makes:

  • Food (agriculture)
  • Things (fabrication, workshops)
  • Energy (solar, small-scale)
  • Services (for the network and beyond)

How it's owned:

  • Members deposit collateral
  • Revenue/resources distributed by smart contract
  • No external shareholders
  • No state enforcement needed

It's a vending machine, but bigger. And you live in it.

One commune is fragile. A network isn't.

What happens when you connect multiple industrial communes?

  • Trade ‚Äî what you make, they need. What they make, you need.
  • Insurance ‚Äî when one has a bad year, the network absorbs it.
  • Mobility ‚Äî when your region gets bad, you can move to another node.
  • Coordination ‚Äî production planning across the network via crypto.

This isn't just sharing. It's a planned economy that no state controls.

The insurance mechanism

Mutual peer insurance. But for your whole life, not just your car.

Communes pool resources into insurance fund
Prediction market (run by communes) tracks regional risks
Human rights index drops? Climate disaster? Political instability?
Insurance kicks in. You relocate to another commune in the network.

The payout isn't money. It's a place to go.

Why start in rural Japan?

8 million empty houses. Villages paying people to move there. Land cheaper than a used car.

But also:

  • Labor shortage ‚Üí visa pathways for agricultural cooperatives
  • High quality of life, low cost of living
  • Not strategically important (empires don't care)
  • Strong infrastructure, weak control

It's a gap. A place where you can build something without anyone stopping you.

Minimum viable post-capitalism

What do you actually need?

A few industrial communes, making real things, coordinating their economies with crypto, insuring each other against collapse.

That's it. That's the skeleton of an economy that doesn't need capitalism to function.
